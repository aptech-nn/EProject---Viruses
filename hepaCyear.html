1989 - Discovery of Hepatitis C Virus

In 1989, scientists identified the hepatitis C virus (HCV), which was responsible for causing chronic hepatitis. Prior to this, hepatitis C was known as non-A, non-B hepatitis.

  • HCV was isolated by researchers at Chiron Corporation.
  • This discovery helped in blood screening and diagnostic tests for HCV.

1990 - Blood Tests for Hepatitis C Introduced

In 1990, the first blood tests for hepatitis C were introduced, allowing for accurate diagnosis and detection of HCV infections.

  • First commercial blood test for HCV approved by the FDA.
  • Blood banks started screening for hepatitis C to ensure safe blood supply.

2000 - Introduction of Combination Therapy

In the early 2000s, combination therapy using interferon and ribavirin became the standard treatment for chronic hepatitis C, offering hope for patients.

  • FDA approves combination therapy for hepatitis C.
  • Successful treatment led to a significant number of patients achieving sustained virological response (SVR).

2011 - Protease Inhibitors Approved

The approval of protease inhibitors such as telaprevir and boceprevir revolutionized the treatment of hepatitis C, improving cure rates and reducing treatment duration.

  • Telaprevir and boceprevir became the first FDA-approved direct-acting antivirals for HCV.
  • Marked a major breakthrough in hepatitis C treatment.

2013 - Sofosbuvir Breakthrough

In 2013, sofosbuvir, a highly effective oral drug, was introduced, marking a significant advancement in hepatitis C treatment by offering shorter treatment durations and fewer side effects.

  • Sofosbuvir approval by the FDA in December 2013.
  • Patients could now achieve cure rates of over 90% with fewer side effects.

2024 - Ongoing Efforts to Eliminate Hepatitis C

As of 2024, hepatitis C remains a focus of global public health efforts, with aims to eliminate the virus by 2030 through better access to treatments, testing, and prevention programs.

  • Global efforts to eliminate hepatitis C by 2030 continue.
  • New antiviral treatments and programs help improve access in low-income regions.
  • Increased awareness about the disease and its effects on liver health.
